Homeless on the Home Front

A Demonstration Program Shows Promise for Ending Veteran's Homelessness

With many soldiers returning from wars in Iraq and Afghanistan in the late 2000s, the risk of homelessness significantly increased for veterans. Looking to avoid the difficulties that plagued so many Vietnam veterans, Congress in 2009 authorized a veterans’ homelessness prevention and intervention demonstration program in support of President Barack Obama’s goal of ending veterans’ homelessness by 2016.

The Labor, Housing and Urban Development, and Veterans’ Affairs departments collaborated to implement an ambitious program that looked to bring innovation to the administration’s fight against veterans homelessness. The resulting program came to be known as the Veterans Homelessness Prevention Demonstration (VHPD).

The Urban Institute evaluated VHPD and found the outcomes for the 509 study participants very promising. To learn more about this innovative program read the research.

Chapter 1: Struggling for Security

After serving more than a dozen years in the Army and the National Guard Reserve, Jannet Taylor, 50, found herself on the edge of homelessness in June 2012. Without warning, life threw a series of tough challenges at her. Taylor needed help, but felt great reluctance asking for it.

Tens of thousands of veterans face similar problems. Translating work experience in the military to the civilian workforce is hard and many veterans say it is difficult to ask for help after coming from a culture that prizes problem solving, self-sufficiency, and doing whatever it takes to complete a mission.

Veterans gather Thursday, Nov. 10, 2011, at the Humboldt Park Armory in Chicago for an event sponsored by the Veteran's Administration. The vets received food, clothing, health screenings, VA and social security benefits and employment counseling. AP Photo/Robert Ray

Veterans are overrepresented in the overall homeless population: while they make up only 8 percent of the total population, they comprise about 11 percent of all homeless adults counted in HUD’s January 2014 point-in-time counts.

VSpc. Karen Arvizu, left, puts on her hydration pack in preparation for her role as a volunteer in a physical demands study, Tuesday, Feb. 25, 2014, in Ft. Stewart, Ga. AP Photo/Stephen B. Morton

Similarly more women veterans are becoming homeless. HUD data show that in 2013, an additional 1,891 women veterans received emergency shelter or transitional housing services.

And homeless women veterans face unique obstacles to stable housing, such as a higher risk of experiencing sexual trauma while in the military.

These trends reflect the large increase in women who joined the military over the last decade. The Government Accountability Office estimates that approximately 1.8 million — or 8 percent — of veterans are now women.

Chapter 2: Seeking Help

Veterans in Central Texas can come to this office at the VA’s Austin Outpatient Clinic to seek help when they are on the verge of losing their home, or are without a place to live. The facility opened in July 2013, more than two years after VHPD was rolled out, and was used as a place to enroll veterans in the demonstration program.

When four-star Army General Eric Shinseki became the seventh VA secretary in 2009, at the top of his to-do list was forming a strategy to combat veterans homelessness and launching a campaign to end it. Several programs came out of that effort. VHPD was one.

VHPD was created to provide housing assistance and supports that keep veterans from losing their homes or placing them in one if necessary. Simultaneously, it aimed to help veterans generate the income and earnings they need to prevent the loss of their homes in the future.

For its part, the VA awarded $5 million to VA grantees to provide case management and outreach services for the VHPD program.

Paul Morigi/AP Images for The National Association of Drug Court Professionals

Led by Secretary Shaun Donovan, HUD also played a prominent role in the administration and execution of VHPD.

Working with the VA and the Labor Department, HUD selected five military bases and their surrounding communities to serve as the proving grounds for VHPD. Each site received $2 million from HUD for outreach and programming.

The Labor Department did not provide specific VHPD funding but still served enrollees through its existing veteran employment programs.

Overall VHPD was implemented in five regions: New York (upstate), San Diego, Tacoma, Tampa Bay, and Texas (central).

The demonstration became one of the first programs to exclusively serve at-risk veterans and their families, and the only one to specifically target post-9/11, women, National Guard, and reserve unit veterans, as well as those with children.

It operated from 2011 to 2014 and served 4,824 adults and children, including 2,024 veterans. The demonstration primarily focused on homelessness prevention, but also used a program known as Rapid Rehousing to help veterans who lost their homes not so long ago.

Between the partner agencies and organizations, a comprehensive suite of services was provided to enrollees, including: housing assistance, health care, and employment services that aimed to put veterans on paths to long term housing sustainability.

President Barack Obama visits with a platoon of U.S. troops at Bagram Air Field in Afghanistan that recently lost six members, Dec. 3, 2010. Official White House Photo by Pete Souza

With less than two months to go until the new year, the campaign to end veterans homelessness is unlikely to reach its goal by the target date. However, the overall initiative has found significant success. According to HUD’s 2014 point-in-time count, the number of homeless veterans is down 33 percent since 2010.

Using the lessons learned during the VHPD program, the Obama administration, the future White House, HUD, the Labor Department, and the VA can continue to reduce the number of homeless veterans.

Chapter 3: Success in Texas

Central Texas is home to 252,000 veterans and VHPD’s success in this region showcases some promising strategies and practices that could aid in the fight to end veterans homelessness.

Within the region’s borders stands Fort Hood, a massive 214,000 acre base that is home to more than 45,000 soldiers and about 9,000 civilian employees, making it the most populated U.S. military base in the world.

Like Jannet Taylor, many veterans who were based at Fort Hood during their time of service stay in the area after leaving the military.

Central Texas was selected as a VHPD demonstration site to find out if this combination of services could work for so many at-risk veterans living over such a wide swath of land.

Covering more than 500 miles of territory in central Texas, VHPD served veterans living in Bell, Coryell, McLennan, Travis, and Williamson counties.

After leaving the Army, Taylor drove a school bus, worked as a teacher’s assistant, served in the National Guard Reserve, and completed her bachelor’s degree in psychology at Tarleton State University, all while raising her daughter on her own.
After completing her degree in 2004, Taylor launched her career in non-profit social service work. But all of that came crashing to a halt when her employer’s grant funding ran out and her position was eliminated.

At risk was Taylor’s home, where she and her daughter had lived since 2011 and where she had fought and beat stage three breast cancer.
But Taylor worked in social services for eight years and was keenly aware of the attitudes that many show towards homeless people seeking help.

Taylor learned of VHPD when she went to the Salvation Army for help with paying her electric bill. While in line, she was given outreach materials for the fledgling demonstration program.
After a friend encouraged her to follow up on the tip, Taylor went to the Veterans Center in Harker Heights and enrolled in VHPD.

From there a case worker provided by the Salvation Army set up short-term financial supports that helped her pay for her rent and utility bills.
The caseworker also sent a letter to Taylor’s landlord explaining what VHPD was and how it was going to ensure her rent got paid on time.
With eviction staved off, Taylor’s next challenge was to find the income she needed to pay for her housing and living expenses.

Taylor worked with her VHPD employment coach to polish her resume, search for jobs, practice for interviews, dress for success, and mentally prepare for the rigors of job hunting and working.
“He was like, ‘you’re dressed, ready to go, ready for a job. Dress like you’re going to work. It will happen,’” Taylor said.

Four months after enrolling in VHPD, Taylor found a job that helped her earn enough money to afford her rent and bills.
Only 19 percent of VHPD participants in Texas had full-time jobs when they entered the program, compared to 41 percent six months to a year after exiting it.
For the majority of veterans served by VHPD - those who could not work due to service-connected disabilities - caseworkers helped navigate them through the system and get them signed up to receive the compensation they earned.

But none of this was possible without boots on the ground.

In Central Texas, the Salvation Army was selected to manage and run the program because of its established presence in all five target counties and its history of working on large scale projects.

The VA partner was the Central Texas Veterans Health Care System, located in Killeen, the same city where Fort Hood lies. The program also involved the Killeen/Heights Vet Center, where outreach workers contacted and enrolled veterans in need.

The Labor Department’s partner was the Texas Veterans Commission, which oversaw veteran-specific employment specialists.

The partners collaborated to create a service plan to meet the needs of veterans in Central Texas and dedicated staff to running the program.

The agencies also worked together to develop a plan for each enrolled veteran, who were required to meet with their case managers from each agency regularly and work the plan.

Chapter 4: A Promising Future

From November 2012 to September 2014, Taylor worked at the VA as an integral part of its Vocational Rehabilitation Therapy program in Temple.
Using her background in the military and social services, as well as her personal experiences, Taylor helped veterans wrestle with the kinds of job-seeking challenges she had faced just months earlier.

“I am in a good place now,” Taylor said. “That program was part of putting me in a good place. Yes, I had some difficult times, but then, I guess without the rain, you can’t enjoy the sunshine.”

Today Taylor works as a VA medical support assistant. Answering phone calls from veterans every day, she helps connect them with the medical professionals and facilities that can treat their physical and mental wounds.
Helping veterans in need makes Taylor feel good because she understands how easily a stroke of bad luck, or past wounds, can hamstring someone who has given so much for their country.

AP Photo/Eric Gay

Going forward the number of post 9/11 and women veterans will inevitably grow after so many enlisted to serve in wars in Afghanistan and Iraq. Data also shows homelessness for these veterans is on the rise. So the challenge remains.

Put another way, even if every single currently homeless veteran found a home tomorrow, new episodes would occur. That is why preventing homelessness is the critical means towards this end.

The good news is federal and local efforts to end veterans homelessness are working.

Furthermore, the Urban Institute’s evaluation of VHPD tells us that identifying veterans for prevention services, attacking the problem before veterans lose their homes, and providing other wraparound services, such as career counseling and daycare are high potential policy options worth further exploration.

And if the lessons from VHPD carry forward into future efforts, then there’s a fighting chance that we can end veterans homelessness and better honor those who have served.
